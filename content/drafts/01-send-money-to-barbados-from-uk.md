## What You Need to Know First: How the GBP/BBD Rate Works

Before you compare providers, there's one thing every Bajan should understand about the Barbadian dollar: **it's fixed**.

The BBD is permanently pegged to the US dollar at exactly 2:1. One US dollar always equals exactly two Barbadian dollars. Always. That never changes.

What *does* change is how many US dollars your British pounds buy — the GBP/USD exchange rate, which floats daily. In March 2026, £1 gets you roughly $1.26 USD, which means approximately **BBD 2.67** per pound.

**Why this matters:** When a transfer company quotes you a GBP/BBD rate, they're essentially showing you their version of the GBP/USD rate, doubled. The mid-market rate is what you'd see on Google or XE.com. Any provider charging you *less* than the mid-market rate is taking a cut of your exchange — their margin, on top of any fees they charge upfront.

Some providers are transparent about this (Wise shows you exactly what margin they charge). Others bury it entirely (Western Union's "no fee" transfers often hide a 4–5% margin in the rate itself).

## The True Cost of a Transfer: Fees vs. Rate Margins

There are two ways providers make money from your transfer:

**1. Upfront fees** — a fixed charge taken before conversion. Easy to see.  
**2. Exchange rate margin** — the gap between the mid-market rate and what they give you. Often hidden.

The cheapest-looking transfer isn't always the cheapest. A provider with £0 upfront fee but a 5% rate margin will cost you far more than one charging £2.50 with a 0.5% margin, especially on larger amounts.

## 5 Tips to Get the Best Rate Every Time

**1. Always compare before you send**  
Rates change daily. What was cheapest last month might not be cheapest today. Use CaribCompare's [live comparison tool](/send-money) to check every time.

**2. Pay by bank transfer, not card**  
Most providers charge extra for debit or credit card payments (often 0.5–1% more). Bank transfer is always cheaper, just slightly slower.

**3. Avoid weekend transfers if rate-sensitive**  
FX markets close on weekends. Some providers apply wider margins on Saturday and Sunday to protect against Monday morning rate moves. If the rate matters most, transfer Monday–Friday during UK business hours.

**4. Send larger amounts less frequently**  
If you're sending £200 every two weeks, consolidating to £400 once a month halves the number of fixed fees you pay. The rate margin stays the same, but you're not paying the upfront fee twice.

**5. Skip the "no fee" marketing**  
"Zero fee" transfers always hide the cost somewhere — usually in a worse exchange rate. The only honest comparison is: how many BBD does my recipient actually get?
